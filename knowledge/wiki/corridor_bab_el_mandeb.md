## Geopolitical Significance
The Bab-el-Mandeb strait is a critical maritime chokepoint connecting the Red Sea to the Gulf of Aden and, by extension, the Indian Ocean. It serves as a strategic link between the Indian Ocean and the Mediterranean Sea via the Red Sea and the Suez Canal. The strait's narrow width necessitates vessels to navigate through the territorial waters of adjacent states, governed by the legal concept of transit passage under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea.

## Chokepoint Characteristics
The Bab-el-Mandeb strait is 26 kilometers (14 nautical miles) wide at its narrowest point, limiting tanker traffic to two 2-mile-wide channels for inbound and outbound shipments. Its choke severity index is 0.55, indicating a significant impact on global energy security if disrupted. Closure of the strait could force tankers originating in the Persian Gulf to divert around the southern tip of Africa, increasing transit time and shipping costs.

## Oil Throughput
The throughput of oil passing through the Bab-el-Mandeb strait is approximately 4.2 million barrels per day (mbpd). This figure represents a substantial portion of the global seaborne-traded petroleum, underscoring the strait's importance in the oil supply chain.
