## Geopolitical Significance
The Bab-el-Mandeb strait is a critical maritime chokepoint connecting the Red Sea to the Gulf of Aden and, by extension, the Indian Ocean. It serves as a strategic link between the Indian Ocean and the Mediterranean Sea via the Red Sea and the Suez Canal. The strait's narrow width necessitates vessels to travel through the territorial sea of adjacent states, governed by the legal concept of transit passage under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea.

## Chokepoint Characteristics
The Bab-el-Mandeb strait is 26 kilometers (14 nautical miles) wide at its narrowest point, limiting tanker traffic to two 2-mile-wide channels for inbound and outbound shipments. Its choke severity index is 0.55, indicating a significant impact on global energy security if closed. The closure of the strait could prevent tankers from the Persian Gulf from transiting the Suez Canal or reaching the SUMED Pipeline, forcing them to divert around the southern tip of Africa, thereby increasing transit time and shipping costs.

## Throughput
The throughput of the Bab-el-Mandeb strait is 4.2 million barrels per day (mbpd) of oil. This figure represents a substantial portion of the global oil supply chain, highlighting the strait's importance in the maritime trade route for petroleum products and liquefied natural gas.

## Location
The Bab-el-Mandeb strait is located at coordinates 12.5°N latitude and 43.3°E longitude, between Yemen on the Arabian Peninsula and Djibouti and Eritrea in the Horn of Africa.
