## Geopolitical and Strategic Importance
The Bab-el-Mandeb strait is a critical maritime chokepoint connecting the Red Sea to the Gulf of Aden and, by extension, the Indian Ocean. It serves as a vital link for global maritime trade, particularly for petroleum products and liquefied natural gas transiting from the Persian Gulf to the Mediterranean Sea via the Suez Canal. The strait's narrow width of 26 kilometers at its narrowest point necessitates vessels to navigate through the territorial waters of adjacent states, making it a focal point for maritime security and geopolitical strategy.

## Chokepoint Characteristics
Bab-el-Mandeb is classified as a significant chokepoint with a choke severity index of 0.55. Its narrow passages limit tanker traffic to two 2-mile-wide channels for inbound and outbound shipments. The closure of this strait could severely impact global energy security by preventing tankers from the Persian Gulf from reaching the Suez Canal or the SUMED Pipeline, thereby forcing them to take the longer route around the southern tip of Africa, which would increase transit time and shipping costs.

## Throughput and Trade Volume
The throughput of the Bab-el-Mandeb strait is approximately 4.2 million barrels per day (mbpd) of oil. This volume represents a substantial portion of the global seaborne-traded petroleum, highlighting the strait's importance in the oil supply chain. The strategic location and high volume of trade make it a critical node in the global energy network.
