## Geopolitical Significance
The Strait of Malacca, located between the Malay Peninsula and the Indonesian island of Sumatra, is a critical maritime corridor connecting the Indian Ocean to the Pacific Ocean. It is one of the world's most important shipping lanes, facilitating the passage of over 94,000 vessels annually. The strait carries approximately 25% of the world's traded goods, including oil, Chinese manufactured products, coal, palm oil, and Indonesian coffee. As of 2024, it handles over 35% of global oil transported by sea and 20% of gas.

## Chokepoint Characteristics
The Strait of Malacca is classified as a severe chokepoint with a choke severity index of 0.70. Its strategic importance is underscored by the high volume of traffic it accommodates, making it a focal point for modern piracy and smuggling activities. The strait's coordinates are approximately 2.5°N latitude and 102.0°E longitude.

## Economic Impact
The economic importance of the Strait of Malacca cannot be overstated. It serves as a vital artery for global trade, particularly for energy resources. The throughput of the strait is approximately 23.0 million barrels per day (mbpd), highlighting its role in the global oil supply chain. The strait's historical significance dates back to the 7th century when regional powers like the Srivijaya empire sought to control it for the spice trade. Subsequent states, including the Malacca Sultanate, the Johor Sultanate, the British Straits Settlements, and the city-state of Singapore, have all vied for dominance over this crucial waterway.
