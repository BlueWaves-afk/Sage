## Geopolitical Significance
The Strait of Malacca is a critical maritime corridor, connecting the Indian Ocean to the Pacific Ocean. It lies between the Malay Peninsula and the Indonesian island of Sumatra. The strait is approximately 930 km long and varies in width from 38 km to 250 km. 

## Economic Importance
The Strait of Malacca is one of the world's most important shipping lanes, facilitating the passage of over 94,000 vessels annually. It carries about 23.0 million barrels per day (mbpd) of oil, representing a significant portion of global oil trade. The strait is vital for the transportation of commodities such as Chinese manufactured products, coal, palm oil, and Indonesian coffee.

## Security Concerns
The high volume of traffic through the Strait of Malacca makes it a target for modern piracy and smuggling activities. The choke severity index of 0.70 indicates a high level of strategic importance and vulnerability. Regional and international cooperation is essential to ensure the security and stability of this vital maritime route.
